Solution 13.2.5.20.1.

  1. Along \(x=\pi\text{,}\) the limit is:

    \begin{align*} \lim_{(x,y)\to(\pi,\pi/2)} \frac{\sin(x) }{\cos(y) } \amp = \lim_{y\to \pi/2} \frac{0}{\cos(y) }\\ \amp = 0\text{.} \end{align*}

  2. Along \(y=x-\pi/2\text{,}\) the limit is:

    \begin{align*} \lim_{(x,y)\to(\pi,\pi/2)} \frac{\sin(x) }{\cos(y) } \amp = \lim_{x\to \pi} \frac{\sin(x) }{\cos(x-\pi/2)}\\ \end{align*}

    Apply L'Hospital's Rule:

    \begin{align*} \amp = \lim_{x\to \pi} \frac{\cos(x) }{\sin(x-\pi/2)}\\ \amp = 1\text{.} \end{align*}

Since the limits along the lines \(x=\pi\) and \(y=x-\pi\) differ, the overall limit does not exist.
