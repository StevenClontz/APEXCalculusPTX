Finally, in Figure 5.4.28.(c) the height of the rectangle is such that the area of the rectangle is exactly that of \(\int_0^4 f(x)\, dx\text{.}\) Since rectangles that are “too big”, as in (a), and rectangles that are “too little,” as in (b), give areas greater/lesser than \(\int_1^4 f(x)\, dx\text{,}\) it makes sense that there is a rectangle, whose top intersects \(f(x)\) somewhere on \([1,4]\text{,}\) whose area is exactly that of the definite integral.
