Volume of a Parallelepiped.

The three dimensional analogue to the parallelogram is the parallelepiped. Each face is parallel to the opposite face, as illustrated in Figure 11.4.17. By crossing \(\vec v\) and \(\vec w\text{,}\) one gets a vector whose magnitude is the area of the base. Dotting this vector with \(\vecu\) computes the volume of parallelepiped! (Up to a sign; take the absolute value.)

The word “parallelepiped” is pronounced “parallel-eh-pipe-ed.”

Thus the volume of a parallelepiped defined by vectors \(\vecu\text{,}\) \(\vecv\) and \(\vec w\) is

\begin{equation} V = \abs{\vecu\cdot (\crossp vw)}\text{.}\tag{11.4.2} \end{equation}

Note how this is the Triple Scalar Product, first seen in Theorem 11.4.6. Applying the identities given in the theorem shows that we can apply the Triple Scalar Product in any “order” we choose to find the volume. That is,

\begin{equation*} V = \abs{\vecu\cdot(\crossp vw)} = \abs{\vec u\cdot (\crossp wv)} = \abs{(\crossp uv)\cdot \vecw}, \text{ etc. } \end{equation*}
