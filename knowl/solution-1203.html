Solution 13.2.5.18.1.

  1. Along \(y=mx\text{,}\) the limit is:

    \begin{align*} \lim_{(x,y)\to(0,0)} \frac{\sin(x^2)}{y} \amp = \lim_{x\to 0} \frac{\sin(x^2)}{mx}\\ \end{align*}

    apply L'Hospital's Rule

    \begin{align*} \amp = \lim_{x\to 0} \frac{2x\cos(x^2)}{m}\\ \amp = 0\text{.} \end{align*}

  2. Along \(x=0\text{,}\) the limit is:

    \begin{equation*} \lim_{(x,y)\to(0,0)} \frac{\sin(x^2)}{y} = \lim_{x\to 0} \frac{\sin(x^2)}{x^2}\text{.} \end{equation*}

    This can be evaluated with L'Hospital's Rule or from known limits; it is 1.

Since the limits along the lines \(y=mx\) are not the same as the limit along \(y=x^2\text{,}\) the overall limit does not exist.
