Solution 13.2.5.19.1.

  1. Along \(y=2\text{,}\) the limit is:

    \begin{align*} \lim_{(x,y)\to(1,2)} \frac{x+y-3}{x^2-1} \amp = \lim_{x\to 1} \frac{x-1}{x^2-1}\\ \amp = \lim_{x\to 1} \frac{1}{x+1}\\ \amp = 1/2\text{.} \end{align*}

  2. Along \(y=x+1\text{,}\) the limit is:

    \begin{align*} \lim_{(x,y)\to(1,2)} \frac{x+y-3}{x^2-1} \amp = \lim_{x\to 1} \frac{2(x-1)}{x^2-1}\\ \amp = \lim_{x\to 1} \frac{2}{x+1}\\ \amp = 1\text{.} \end{align*}

Since the limits along the lines \(y=2\) and \(y=x+1\) differ, the overall limit does not exist.
